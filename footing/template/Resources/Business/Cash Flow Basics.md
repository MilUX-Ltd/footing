# Cash Flow Basics

Profit is an opinion; cash is a fact. A business can be profitable on paper and still run out of money, because profit counts revenue when it's earned, not when it's actually in the bank. This page is the minimum discipline for not being surprised by your own bank balance.

## What to track

- **Cash in the bank, today.** Check it at the same point each week. The number matters less than the habit of looking.
- **What's owed to you (debtors) and when it's due.** An invoice sent isn't cash. An invoice paid is.
- **What you owe (creditors) and when it's due.** Suppliers, HMRC, payroll, rent. Know the dates before they arrive.
- **The gap between the two.** If what's owed to you is consistently slower to land than what you owe is due to leave, you have a structural cash problem even if the business is otherwise healthy.

Weekly is the right cadence for a small operation. Monthly is too slow to catch a problem while it's still cheap to fix.

## A simple runway calculation

Runway is how long you can keep operating if income stopped tomorrow.

```
Runway (months) = Cash in the bank ÷ Average monthly outgoings
```

Use your last three months of actual outgoings, not a budget, for the denominator. A budget tells you what you planned to spend; outgoings tell you what you actually did. Recalculate monthly. Watch the trend, not just the number: a shrinking runway is the thing that matters, not the runway itself at any single point.

## Early warning signs

None of these are emergencies on their own. Two or more at once, moving in the same direction, is worth a proper look.

- **Debtor days creeping out.** Clients who used to pay in 30 days start paying in 45, then 60. This is often the first sign of trouble, either yours or theirs.
- **One client becoming too much of the total.** If a single client is more than a third of your revenue, their payment terms are effectively your cash-flow policy, whether you chose that or not.
- **The gap between invoicing and banking widens.** You're doing the same amount of work but seeing the money later.
- **Reaching for credit to cover routine outgoings.** Using a credit facility for payroll or rent, rather than for planned investment, is a sign the underlying gap needs fixing, not financing.
- **You stop checking the number.** If looking at the bank balance has started to feel uncomfortable, that discomfort is information. Look anyway.

## What this page is not

Not accounting advice, not a bookkeeping system, not a substitute for an accountant or a proper set of management accounts once the business is big enough to need them. It's the floor: the habits that mean cash-flow trouble shows up as a trend you can act on, rather than a surprise.
