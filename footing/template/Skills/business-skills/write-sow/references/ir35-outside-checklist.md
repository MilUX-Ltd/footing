# IR35 outside-status checklist for the SOW

What the SOW must show, factor by factor, to support an outside-IR35 position, plus the language that quietly puts an engagement inside. A drafting aid, not a status determination. Apply it in Step 4. UK limited-company contractors only.

## The governing reality, stated plainly

- **Status is a question of fact, not of wording alone.** A clean SOW helps; a contract that says one thing while the working relationship does another counts for little. Where the facts point inside, flag it for the risk register rather than papering over it with words.
- **For medium and large clients the end client decides.** Under the off-payroll working rules, a medium or large client makes the status determination and issues a Status Determination Statement (SDS). Your company, as the supplier, does not get to declare the engagement outside. The SOW shapes the facts the client assesses; it does not replace the SDS.
- **For small clients you decide.** Where the end client is a small company, the original IR35 rules apply and your own company is responsible for the status assessment. Either way the same factors decide it.
- **HMRC weighs the whole picture.** There is no pass-mark of "two out of three". Control and personal service / substitution carry the most weight, with financial risk and being in business on your own account close behind.

## Factor 1: Control (heaviest factor)

Who decides what, how, when and where the work is done. Outside IR35 wants your company controlling the how, when and where; the client controls only the what (the outcome).

SOW must show: you determine your own method, tools, sequencing and working hours; you work to deliverables and milestones, not a fixed weekly attendance pattern or client rota; no line management of you by the client, and no requirement to seek permission for how the work is done.

Red-flag language to remove or rewrite: "X days per week", "on site Mondays and Wednesdays", "during core hours", "as directed by the project manager", "will report to", "will attend daily stand-ups as a team member", "the client will allocate tasks". Rewrite "three days a week on the design" as "the design deliverables defined in section X, delivered by the milestone dates in section Y, with the supplier determining its own working pattern".

## Factor 2: Substitution (personal service)

Whether you must do the work personally, or can send a qualified substitute. A genuine, exercisable right to substitute is a strong outside pointer. If you have associates or a network you can call on, this is real, not paper.

SOW must show: the supplier may provide a suitably qualified substitute to perform the services, at the supplier's cost, without needing client consent (the client may verify competence and security clearance, but cannot reject a cleared, competent substitute); the contract is with your company for an outcome, not with you personally.

Red-flag language: "[your name] will personally perform", "the named individual", "key personnel may not be changed without consent" with no substitution carve-out. Security-clearance and SC/DV gating is legitimate and can stay, but must not become a back-door personal-service clause.

## Factor 3: Mutuality of obligation (MOO)

Whether the client must offer ongoing work and you must accept it. Note the current HMRC position: where work is done for payment, HMRC argues a contract exists and so a baseline MOO is always present. MOO is therefore the weakest lever; do not rely on it. What still helps is showing the engagement is one defined piece of work, not a standing arrangement.

SOW must show: a defined scope with a start and an end tied to deliverables, not a rolling commitment; no obligation on the client to provide further work after the deliverables, and no obligation on you to accept further work without a new SOW; no notice-period-as-employment, no guaranteed minimum hours or income.

Red-flag language: "ongoing support", "rolling monthly", "minimum of N days guaranteed", "the contractor will be available for further tasks as required".

## Factor 4: Financial risk / in business on own account

Whether your company carries genuine commercial risk and behaves as a business. This is the factor outcome-based pricing exists to satisfy.

SOW must show: fixed-price or capped pricing against outcomes, so you can lose money on overruns and keep the upside on efficiency; you fix defects in your own time and at your own cost (a defect-remedy clause); you use your own equipment and methods, carry your own professional indemnity and public liability insurance, and are responsible for your own training and overheads.

Red-flag language: pure day-rate with no cap and no deliverable acceptance, expenses-plus-time billing with no risk transfer, client providing all equipment and indemnifying you against your own errors.

## Factor 5: Part and parcel of the organisation

Whether you are integrated into the client like an employee.

SOW must show: you are an external supplier, with no staff benefits, no line-management role, not on the internal org chart, and not holding a permanent pass or internal title as a team member.

Red-flag language: "will manage the client's team", "will hold the role of", "entitled to", anything that reads as a position rather than a service. A client security pass for site access is fine and necessary; an internal job title is not.

## Factor 6: Equipment and other pointers

You provide your own equipment and software where practical (client-owned secure environments for regulated or sensitive work are a legitimate exception; note it as a security necessity, not a control concession). You can work for other clients during the engagement (no exclusivity unless commercially justified and time-boxed). You correct substandard work at your own expense.

## CEST and professional review

For higher-value or borderline engagements, run the engagement through HMRC's Check Employment Status for Tax (CEST) tool against the real working terms, not just the SOW wording. HMRC stands by a CEST result where the inputs reflect the actual relationship, though CEST returns "undetermined" in a meaningful minority of cases. Take professional review (accountant or IR35 specialist) where the engagement is high value, long, or shows mixed indicators.

## Disclaimer text to carry into the SOW

> This Statement of Work has been drafted to reflect an outcome-based, supplier engagement consistent with an outside-IR35 position. It is not a determination of employment status for tax. Where the end client is a medium or large organisation, the client is responsible for determining status under the off-payroll working rules and issuing a Status Determination Statement. Status is ultimately a question of fact about the working relationship. The parties should ensure the actual working practices match the terms set out here.
