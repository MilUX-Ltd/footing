# Outcome-based SOW structure

The section order and the rules for writing each one. The fillable version is `assets/sow-template.md`.

## The principle: outcome, not activity

An outcome-based SOW defines what must be true when the work is done and lets the supplier decide how to get there. Every deliverable answers two questions: what is the result, and how will we both know it has been achieved. If a section describes your presence, hours, or day-to-day tasks instead of a result, it is written wrong and it weakens the outside-IR35 position. Rewrite it.

Rule of thumb for scope size: roughly three core outcomes, three primary deliverables, and three KPIs per workstream. It keeps acceptance writable and governance manageable. If the engagement needs more, split it into phases, each with its own deliverables and acceptance.

## Sections

1. **Document control.** Client, your company, SOW reference, version, date, the MSA or framework it sits under (or "standalone"), named signatories.

2. **Background and purpose.** Two or three sentences: the client's situation and the outcome they are buying. Client's own words where the input gives them. No filler.

3. **Engagement outcomes.** The two to four measurable changes you are delivering, stated as results. Each is the headline that the deliverables below it serve. This is the heart of an outcome-based SOW.

4. **Scope of services and deliverables.** For each deliverable: a unique reference (D1, D2, ...); the deliverable as a result, not a task; its acceptance criteria (objective, checkable, agreed before work starts, see `metrics-library.md`); the format and how it is handed over; which engagement outcome it serves.

5. **Out of scope.** An explicit list of what you are not doing. Be generous. This is the primary defence against scope creep and a clean signal of a bounded engagement (helps the MOO position too).

6. **Acceptance process.** Who reviews, who has sign-off authority, the review window (e.g. five working days), what counts as accepted, and what happens on rejection (one round of remedy within scope; further rounds are change control). Deemed acceptance if the client does not respond within the window.

7. **Approach and method (high level).** Enough to show you are bringing your own method, without committing to hours or attendance. Reinforces control sitting with the supplier. Keep it to the what and why, not a timesheet.

8. **Supplier responsibilities.** What you provide: people, method, equipment, insurance (professional indemnity and public liability), correction of defects at your own cost.

9. **Client responsibilities and dependencies.** Access, data, people, decisions, environments the client must provide and by when. Each one with a date. State that delivery dates and price assume these are met, and that slippage is handled under change control. Every dependency here is also a risk in the register.

10. **Timeline and milestones.** Phase or milestone dates against deliverables. Not a weekly cadence of attendance.

11. **Commercials.** Pricing model (fixed-price per outcome / day-rate capped / milestone-staged), the price or estimate band, payment schedule tied to milestones or acceptance, expenses policy, VAT, and the basis of estimate. Contingency shown where you choose to expose it.

12. **Change control.** Any change to scope, deliverables, dependencies or timeline is captured in a written change note, costed, and agreed before the work proceeds. Prevents free scope expansion and protects the fixed price. This clause is doing real commercial work; do not water it down.

13. **Term and termination.** Start, end tied to deliverables, notice, what happens to part-completed and accepted work on termination. Avoid employment-style notice framing.

14. **IP and confidentiality.** Who owns what, what licence the other party gets, confidentiality (or a pointer to the NDA / MSA). Carry your standard position; if absent from the input, ask.

15. **Status and IR35 note.** The disclaimer from `ir35-outside-checklist.md`.

16. **Signatures.** Your company's signatory and the client's authorised signatory. Execution itself is done through your e-signature tool, not this skill.

17. **Annex A: Risk register (optional).** Internal by default; include in the client PDF only if you choose to.

## Writing acceptance criteria

Good acceptance criteria are objective, measurable, and verifiable without argument. Test each one: could two reasonable people disagree about whether it was met? If yes, tighten it.

- Weak: "improve the onboarding experience". Strong: "redesigned onboarding flow delivered as annotated wireframes covering the five journeys in D1, validated in moderated testing with at least six target users, with task completion at or above 80% on the three priority tasks".
- Weak: "deliver training". Strong: "two half-day workshops delivered to up to twelve named participants, with a facilitator pack and participant workbook, and a post-session confidence measure showing a stated increase against the pre-session baseline on the four target competencies".

## Converting a job spec into outcomes

Job specs are written as roles and hours, which is an inside-IR35 shape. Translate: "3 days/week UX designer" becomes the design deliverables and their acceptance criteria, by milestone, with you setting your own pattern; "report to the delivery lead" becomes "the supplier coordinates with the named client contact for access and acceptance" (coordination, not subordination); "ongoing support" becomes a defined deliverable with an end, plus an optional priced extension under a new SOW.
