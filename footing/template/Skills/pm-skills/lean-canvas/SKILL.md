## What this is, and when to reach for it

A fast, hypothesis-driven one-page business model for a new venture. It is a brainstorming and risk-surfacing tool, not a strategy document. When a founder needs both strategic clarity and a business model, use `startup-canvas`; when they need the full strategy, use `product-strategy`.

## The canvas

- **Problem**, the top three customer problems, and the current unsatisfactory solutions.
- **Solution**, the top three features or approaches.
- **Unique value proposition**, one memorable line; why they choose you (use `value-proposition` for the detail).
- **Unfair advantage**, what cannot be easily copied.
- **Customer segments**, the early adopters and first segment; purpose-led, customer and user distinct.
- **Channels**, how you reach them; the route to contract.
- **Revenue streams**, how you make money; pricing model; lifetime value.
- **Cost structure**, fixed and variable costs; cost to acquire.
- **Key metrics**, activation, retention, revenue; the criteria the customer actually cares about.

## Method

Draft each box, then attack the riskiest assumptions first and suggest cheap tests (interviews, a landing page, a small pilot). Update as the founder learns. Render the canvas for them to react to.

## Notes

- The upstream author flags the Lean Canvas limitations honestly: Problem overlaps Segments, Solution overlaps Value Proposition, and it carries no vision, trade-offs or relative-costs section. Use it for speed; reach for `startup-canvas` or `product-strategy` when those gaps matter.
- Pairs with `business-model`, `startup-canvas`, `pricing-strategy`, `value-proposition`.
