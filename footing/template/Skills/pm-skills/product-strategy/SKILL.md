## What this is

A nine-section canvas for setting the strategy of an offering: how it competes, wins and grows. It serves your own service-line strategy and product strategy work.

## The canvas

1. **Vision**, what you aspire to, and the values you hold. Keep it simple; it evolves with the strategy.
2. **Market segments**, purpose-named segments (the job customers hire the offering for), not demographics; the customer who selects and the user who operates. Which segment first, and why.
3. **Relative costs**, low-cost position or unique-value position. Low cost is not the same as low price.
4. **Value proposition**, per segment, the what-before, how, what-after, alternatives, expressed as how well the offering fits the customer's needs (use the `value-proposition` skill for the detail).
5. **Trade-offs**, what you will not do. Saying no creates focus.
6. **Key metrics**, the criteria you run against, a north-star and the one metric that matters this quarter. Tie metrics to real customer outcomes, not vanity numbers.
7. **Growth**, the route to market and the channels; where selling to larger organisations, the route to contract and the procurement picture.
8. **Capabilities**, what you must build or partner for to win.
9. **Defensibility**, why a competitor cannot or will not copy the integrated set of choices.

## Method

Work the nine in order, then check coherence: do the elements reinforce each other? Surface the hypotheses that must be true, and suggest cheap experiments to test the riskiest. Revisit as conditions change. Render the canvas visually to react to.

## Market structure and Wardley mapping

Ground the canvas in the structure of the market: how concentrated the buyer base is, the tier of incumbents, any accreditation or regulatory barriers, the gap between pilot and scaled contract where relevant, and current sector direction. For situational awareness and anticipation, complement the canvas with **Wardley mapping** (Simon Wardley): plot the value chain against component evolution to see where the offering sits, where incumbents hold position, and where commoditisation erodes a moat. It sharpens the segments, trade-offs and defensibility sections.
