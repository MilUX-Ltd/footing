# Porter's Five Forces

## What this is

A structural read of an industry's attractiveness and the forces that set profitability. One of a market-scan set. It is most valuable where a small number of buyers or incumbents dominate a market, but the same five forces apply to any sector.

## The five forces

1. **Competitive rivalry**, incumbents and specialist challengers; the pace of demand (fast-moving or slow and contract-driven); differentiation through capability, accreditation or brand rather than price alone.
2. **Supplier power**, key suppliers, single-source or scarce inputs, switching costs, concentration in the supply chain.
3. **Buyer power**, how concentrated and powerful the buyer base is. Where one or a small number of large customers dominate, they hold strong bargaining power, run structured competitions, and set the route to contract on their terms. Selling through an intermediary or channel partner shifts, but does not remove, this.
4. **Threat of substitutes**, alternative approaches, in-house build, doing nothing, or a different way of solving the underlying problem that removes the need.
5. **Threat of new entrants**, bounded by barriers such as accreditation or certification, regulatory approval, capital requirements, incumbent relationships, and the time and cost to cross from a pilot to a scaled, funded contract.

## Method

Rate each force (high, medium, low) with its current state and trend; judge overall attractiveness; pick the two or three forces most critical to the strategy; develop responses (reduce the high-power forces, exploit the weak ones); identify positioning opportunities. Render the result.

## Notes

- Where a market has a small number of dominant buyers and an established tier of incumbents, the buyer-power and new-entrant forces are usually the structural story worth naming explicitly.
- No market is attractive or unattractive in the abstract; position decides it.
- Pairs with `swot-analysis`, `pestle-analysis`, `ansoff-matrix`, `competitor-analysis`.
