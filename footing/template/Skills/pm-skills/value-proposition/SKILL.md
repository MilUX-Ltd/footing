## What this is

You design the value proposition *with* the customer. The 6-part template is kept because it is good and customer-first. The reframing is to express the value as how well the offering fits what the customer is really selecting on, so the proposition and any later assessment speak the same language.

## One segment at a time

A value proposition is segment-specific. Run one pass per segment. Distinguish the **customer** value (why the person who selects and pays would choose this: total cost of ownership, risk, accreditation or compliance where relevant, route to contract) from the **user** value (why the person who uses it day to day is better off). They are often different propositions to different people.

## The 6-part template

You are designing a value proposition for **$ARGUMENTS**.

1. **Who**, the segment, and within it the customer or user this proposition addresses. Their characteristics and constraints.
2. **Why (purpose)**, the job they are hiring the offering for, and the outcome they want. State what they are selecting on.
3. **What before**, their current situation; what they use today; the friction and where it falls short of what they need.
4. **How**, how the offering meets the purpose; the specific capabilities that move each thing the customer cares about; why it clears the bar where the alternative does not.
5. **What after**, the improved state; what becomes possible. Note anything where you would **overserve**, since that is cost the customer may not value.
6. **Alternatives**, what they would use without you (incumbent, in-house, do nothing), and why you are the better fit. Name the switching cost.

Finish with a one to two sentence value proposition statement, and a positioning statement ready for proposal, marketing and onboarding use.

## Worked example

- **Who:** an operations lead procuring a scheduling tool for a distributed field-service team.
- **Why:** keep the team's diary reliable without a full-time coordinator; they select on ease of use, fits existing systems, low training burden.
- **What before:** a shared spreadsheet and phone calls; slow, error-prone, no shared picture.
- **How:** runs on existing devices, integrates with the current booking system, minimal training.
- **What after:** a shared, reliable schedule; faster, fewer errors; clears the integration bar the alternative could not.
- **Alternatives:** an established provider's heavier system (costlier, longer to roll out) or status quo (free, but unfit). Switching cost is training and data migration, offset within one quarter.

## This template versus Strategyzer

The 6-part template starts with the customer and works toward the solution, handles one segment per pass, forces an explicit Alternatives section, and produces a reusable statement. Use Strategyzer's Value Proposition Canvas when you need a detailed pains and gains decomposition for a mature, complex offering; use this for clarity, speed and an actionable output.

## The multi-stakeholder proposition

Many purchases, especially in B2B or public-sector selling, are judged by several stakeholders (the end user, the budget holder, procurement or commercial), each with a different job. Blending their propositions dilutes the message. Write one tailored proposition per audience, mapped onto the Value Proposition Canvas (jobs/pains/gains against products/pain-relievers/gain-creators). A clear proposition is also the antidote to failure demand: work created because the customer's real need was not met the first time.

## Notes

- The stronger the value proposition, the easier the bid, the marketing and the product decisions.
- Test propositions with real customers and users before finalising.
- A Value Curve (Blue Ocean) is a good visual to compare the offering against alternatives across the criteria the customer selects on.
- Pairs with `user-personas`, `ideal-customer-profile`, `competitor-analysis`.
