# Beachhead Segment

Forked from [phuryn/pm-skills](https://github.com/phuryn/pm-skills) v2.0.0 (`pm-go-to-market/beachhead-segment`, MIT, copyright Pawel Huryn). Based on Geoffrey Moore's beachhead strategy in *Crossing the Chasm*. MIT licence text retained in `LICENSE` alongside this file. **Modified by MilUX:** adapted for general business use and reframed in plain language for public release; the original method and attribution are retained. Modifications copyright 2026 MilUX Ltd, released under the same MIT licence.

## What this is

You choose the beachhead. It follows segmentation and the ICP, and feeds the value proposition and the go-to-market plan.

## The four criteria

1. **Acute, unmet need.** Does this segment carry a real gap or pain, getting worse, with fragile or expensive workarounds? The burning pain is usually an operational shortfall or a mandated requirement, not a productivity annoyance.

2. **Ability to contract.** Is there budget and a route to put it under contract? A named budget line, a clear value case, a sponsor with influence over the decision, and a procurement or buying route you can actually use. Willingness to pay alone is not enough if there is no route to contract. **Qualify the funding explicitly: interest is not budget authority.** A warm meeting, a trial, or a pilot is not a funded route. Establish whether the effort is actually funded, at what order of magnitude, and on what timeframe; and distinguish genuine exposure or early interest from a gatekeeper who controls the budget and the requirement.

3. **Winnability.** Can you realistically win and hold this segment? Read the incumbent, the established players in the category, the differentiation, and the distribution or access advantage. Be honest about the gap between a promising pilot and being awarded ongoing budget: a segment where you can win a trial but cannot cross to a scaled, funded contract is not yet winnable.

4. **Referral into adjacent segments.** Will winning here open the next one? Most sectors are smaller and more networked than they look; a reference customer, a successful pilot, or an adjacent buyer who watches this one is the expansion path.

## Method

1. **List candidate segments** from the segmentation work: purpose-named, not firmographic buckets.
2. **Validate the need** in each with evidence (interviews, requirements, published data), and quantify the impact of the gap.
3. **Assess the route to contract**: budget, sponsor, procurement route, typical deal shape and timeline.
4. **Evaluate winnability**: incumbents, differentiation, and the pilot-to-contract risk.
5. **Map referral pathways**: adjacent segments, the sector's network, reference potential.
6. **Choose the beachhead**: highest combined score, most achievable now, shortest honest path to a contract and a reference, best springboard to the next segment.

## Output

Top three to five segments scored against the four criteria; the recommended beachhead with the evidence behind it; a go-to-market plan for it; and the expansion path to adjacent segments once it is held.

## Notes

- Start specific. A narrow, winnable beachhead beats a vague large market.
- Do not plan to leave the beachhead until it is genuinely held and referenceable.
- Pairs with `ideal-customer-profile`, `market-sizing`, `value-proposition`, and `strategy-red-team` (stress-test the choice).
