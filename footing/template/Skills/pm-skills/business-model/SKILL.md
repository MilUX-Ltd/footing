## What this is, and when to reach for it

The most widely used canvas: a full view of how value is created, delivered and captured across nine blocks. It is the right tool for an established business, a corporate strategy, or investor materials, where articulating how the operational pieces connect is the point. It is distinct from `lean-canvas` (a faster, startup-focused, hypothesis tool) and from `startup-canvas` (which separates strategy from model for a new product). The blocks differ, so the artefacts are not interchangeable; pick by where the organisation actually is.

## The nine blocks

- **Key partners**, strategic partners and suppliers; the incumbents and established players who hold the channels you need.
- **Key activities**, the critical processes that deliver value.
- **Key resources**, assets, IP, people, finance; accreditations or certifications count here where relevant.
- **Value propositions**, the value to each segment (use `value-proposition` for the detail).
- **Customer relationships**, how relationships are established and kept.
- **Channels**, how customers discover, buy, receive and are supported; the route to purchase.
- **Customer segments**, purpose-led, customer and user kept distinct.
- **Cost structure**, fixed and variable; the cost drivers.
- **Revenue streams**, how money is made; pricing mechanisms; unit economics.

## Method

Profile segments first, define the value propositions, map relationships and channels, list activities, resources and partners, then cost and revenue. Check all nine align, and test viability (lifetime value comfortably above acquisition cost). Render the canvas.

## Notes

- The author flags the BMC limitations: no vision, no defensibility test, no trade-offs, no metrics, and the partner/resource blocks are thin for early-stage products. For those, use `startup-canvas` or `product-strategy`.
- Pairs with `lean-canvas`, `startup-canvas`, `pricing-strategy`. For revenue-model patterns, see `monetization-strategy`.
